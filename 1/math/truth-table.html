!!!

\(p\) = “you win”
\(q\) = “I give you money”
\(p\to q\) = “if you win then I give you money.”
What would make this statement false? i.e. \(\neg (p \to q)\)?
Well, it is false only when, “if you win and then I don’t give you money”
\(\therefore\) “If you lose and I give you money”, the statement is still ok! I didn’t break any promise!

!!!

a conditional is equivalent to its contrapositive.
\(\neg q \to \neg p\) = “If I didn’t give you money then you must not have won”
now look back to truth table of \(p \to q\) and notice that if p then not q is false. Block that line and read the table, which states that the if you didn’t money then you must not have won base from the condition. So (\(\neg q \to \neg p\)) is a true statement.

Can you do the same to prove why a conditional is not equal to its inverse or converse? i.e. prove the statement would be false.
